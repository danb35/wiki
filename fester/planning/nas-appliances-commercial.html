Dad jokes aside, if you:

  • Aren't technical enough to build a server; or
  • You just want to get something working fast (and reasonably cheap);

and

  • The hardware limitations of pre-built NAS boxes are OK for your needs
    • Numbers of easily accessed 3.5" disk slots (HDDs) - typically between 2 & 5 slots for home / small offices or up to 12 slots for larger offices.
    • Extra internal slots for SSDs (USB, M.2, NVME)
    • CPU architecture & power - for TrueNAS the architecture needs to be x64 Intel / AMD
    • GPU for media transcoding (may require paid subscription to e.g. Plex for GPU transcoding - CPU transcoding is normally free)
    • Memory - ECC? Maximum size?
    • Networking - no. of ports, speeds
    • Electricity consumption

then this might well be the best solution for you.

You can either buy new, or look to eBay (other similar sites are available) for a significantly cheaper 2nd user box.

Some of the major brands to look for:

  • ixSystems - yes, them, the developers of TrueNAS make NAS appliances
  • Synology
  • TerraMaster
  • QNAP - but watch out for their smaller appliances which often use ARM processors or max out at 4GB of memory.
  • etc.

(See here and here for a bigger list of NAS brands.)

Some key points to look for:

  1. These brands usually come with NAS software (ixSystems boxes come with TrueNAS, obviously) but you can run TrueNAS instead if it is a better match to your needs than the brand software.
  2. But NAS software based on ZFS (rather than BTFS) has significantly higher memory requirements and even if you can upgrade the memory the maximum may not be enough. Similarly if you want to run big apps or virtual machines, maximum memory may be an issue.
  3. Many / most do not have ECC memory.
  4. Most do have proper drive bays - where drives sit in caddies which slide into slots and lock in and this makes it possible to change a drive without opening the case.
  5. Many do not have hot-swap - in other words you cannot change out a drive whilst the server is operating but instead will need to power it down to change the drive.
  6. Most but not all will have Gigabit Ethernet or faster which is normally good enough for a home / small office environment. 

    Note: Gigabit or faster ethernet is only worth having if your network is designed to support it i.e. all the equipment between the NAS and your key Wifi Access point(s) is the same fast speed all the way which means switches and wireless access points all need to support the same e.g. Gigabit Ethernet, and you also need to have Wifi5 (803.11AC) or later on your key access points and your key devices. However, if you don't already have it you will be amazed at just how much difference and upgrade to Wifi5 or later will make to your wifi speeds.
  7. Almost all new NAS boxes and priced without disks - you will need to budget to buy the disks you want separately.
  8. When you buy disks, try to buy disks which are designed for NAS or enterprise usage like Seagate Ironwold or Ironwolf Pro rather than consumer disks which are not designed to run 24x7 or in close proximity to one another. Check carefully the type of technology in the disk - and avoid SMC technology disks because their write performance characteristics will be very poor and they will be prone to earlier failure. A cheap way of getting disks is to buy standalone external disks in a USB case, and “shuck” them (which means take out the disk and throwing the case away - beware doing this as these can be whatever disks happen to be cheap and available and are likely to be SMC technology.
  9. If you are buying a used disks, either standalone or as part of a used NAS which comes with disks, then you should try to get the vendor to supply you with SMART reports for each disk so that you can see the wear status of the disks (model, serial number, spinning hours, power cycles, spin-up cycles, head retractions, errors, bad sectors etc.), and the substantially reduced price cf. new needs to factor in that these disks will almost certainly be out of warranty and more likely to fail.
